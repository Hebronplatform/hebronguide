📍 파이크 플레이스 마켓
1907년에 문을 연 이 마켓은 미국에서 가장 오래된 파머스 마켓입니다. 중간 상인 없이 농부에게 직접 사고 싶었던 시민들의 작은 반란이 지금까지 이어진 겁니다. 저 생선 던지는 퍼포먼스는 1988년부터 시작됐어요.
파이크 플레이스 마켓은 1907년 8월 17일, 시민들의 작은 반란으로 시작됐습니다. 당시 채소 한 단을 사려면 중간 상인을 두세 명 거쳐야 했고, 그러다 보니 가격이 세 배로 뛰었어요. 화가 난 시민들이 시의회에 청원을 넣었고, 농부가 직접 파는 시장이 탄생했습니다. 첫날 농부 열 명이 나왔는데 오전에 물건이 다 팔렸고, 군중이 너무 많아 경찰이 출동할 정도였어요.

입구에 있는 청동 돼지 '레이첼' — 1986년부터 자리를 지키는 저금통이에요. 동전을 넣으면 꿀꿀 소리가 나고, 그 돈은 마켓 내 저소득층 사업자와 사회복지 프로그램에 쓰입니다. 지금까지 모은 돈이 수십만 달러예요.

그리고 저 생선 던지는 분들 — 파이크 플레이스 피쉬 마켓. 1988년, 무거운 생선을 매일 들고 나르는 게 힘들어서 '그냥 던지자'고 시작했어요. 관광객들이 환호하자 퍼포먼스가 됐고, 지금은 하버드 비즈니스 스쿨에서 직장 문화 사례 연구로 쓰는 유명한 이야기가 됐습니다. 일을 즐기면 무엇이든 달라진다는 것, 이 마켓이 가장 잘 보여주고 있어요.
📍 껌 벽 (Post Alley)
1993년부터 시작됐어요. 근처 극장 손님들이 껌을 붙이기 시작했는데 청소해도 소용없자 시에서 포기했습니다. 막을 수 없으면 명물로 만들면 된다는 시애틀의 철학이에요.
이 벽은 1993년에 시작됐어요. 바로 저쪽 '마켓 씨어터' 손님들이 줄 서면서 지루함에 껌을 붙이기 시작했습니다. 시에서 두 번이나 고압 세척기로 청소했지만, 청소한 날 저녁부터 사람들이 다시 붙이러 왔어요. 결국 포기하고 명물로 인정했습니다.

지금 이 벽에 붙은 껌이 몇 개인지 세어봤을까요? 수백만 개예요. 심지어 2015년에 한 번 완전 청소를 했는데 — 껌 무게가 약 1톤이나 됐대요. 그리고 청소 당일 저녁부터 다시 쌓이기 시작했습니다.

어떤 사람들은 껌에 소원을 써서 붙이고, 어떤 커플은 기념으로 하트 모양을 만들고, 어떤 아이들은 자기 껌으로 그림을 그려요. 막을 수 없는 인간의 창의성 — 이 벽이 그 증거입니다.
📍 스페이스 니들
1962년 세계박람회를 위해 400일 만에 지었습니다. 목표는 '21세기처럼 보이는 건물'. 60년이 지난 지금도 미래적으로 보이죠? 높이 184m, 시속 320km 바람에도 견딥니다.
1962년 세계박람회를 위해 단 400일 만에 지어진 스페이스 니들. 설계자들에게 주어진 미션은 딱 하나였어요 — "21세기처럼 보여야 한다." 실제로 21세기가 되었을 때, 그리고 지금도 여전히 미래적으로 보인다는 게 놀랍죠?

높이는 184미터. 무게는 9,550톤인데, 그중 절반이 기초 콘크리트예요. 왜냐하면 이 지역이 지진대이거든요. 시속 320킬로미터 바람에도 견디게 설계됐고, 규모 9.1 지진에도 버틸 수 있습니다. 2018년에는 맨 위 전망 바닥을 유리로 교체해서 발아래 시애틀이 그대로 보입니다.

꼭대기에 회전 레스토랑이 있어요 — 47분에 한 바퀴 돕니다. 1962년에 열었을 때 미국 최초의 회전 레스토랑이었어요. 지금은 그냥 전망대만 올라가도 360도 파노라마가 펼쳐지는데, 맑은 날엔 레이니어 산, 올림픽 반도, 캐나다 밴쿠버 섬까지 보입니다.
📍 아마존 스피어스
아마존이 왜 유리 구형 건물을 지었을까요? "열대우림 속에서 일하면 창의력이 올라간다"는 철학으로, 4만 종 열대식물이 있는 사무실을 만든 겁니다.
아마존 스피어스는 2018년 문을 열었어요. 세 개의 유리 구 안에 40,000여 종의 열대·아열대 식물이 살고 있습니다. 아마존 직원들의 일상적인 업무 공간이자 협업 장소예요. 지금 이 안에서 누군가가 프레젠테이션을 하고 있거나, 코드를 짜고 있거나, 회의를 하고 있을 거예요 — 열대우림 한가운데서요.

왜 이런 건물을 지었을까요? 제프 베이조스가 확신했던 것이 있어요 — 자연 속에 있으면 사람의 창의력이 올라간다는 것. 그래서 아마존은 단순한 사무실 대신, 직원들이 숲 속에 있는 것 같은 환경을 만들었습니다. 에콰도르 열대우림에서 온 나무, 마다가스카르에서 온 식물, 아마존 강 유역에서 가져온 수생식물까지 있어요.

건물 관리팀이 있어서 식물들의 습도, 온도, 물 공급을 24시간 모니터링합니다. 식물을 위한 IT 시스템이에요. 어떻게 보면 아마존다운 생각이죠 — 세상에서 가장 정교한 물류를 구축한 회사가 이번엔 실내 열대우림 물류를 만든 겁니다.
📍 케리 공원 (황금시간대)
시애틀 엽서 사진의 95%가 여기서 찍힙니다. 스페이스 니들 뒤로 레이니어 산이 보이는 구도 — 해질 무렵 하늘이 분홍빛으로 물들 때 비로소 'Emerald City'가 이해됩니다.
케리 공원은 퀸 앤 언덕 위에 있는 작은 공원이에요. 사실 이름만 '공원'이지, 잔디밭 조금에 난간 하나가 전부입니다. 그런데 이 공원이 시애틀에서 가장 유명한 장소 중 하나예요. 왜냐면 여기서 찍히는 구도가 있거든요 — 스페이스 니들을 중심으로, 좌측엔 다운타운 고층 빌딩들, 우측엔 퓨젯 사운드, 그리고 맑은 날엔 배경에 레이니어 산. 시애틀 엽서의 95%가 이 구도에서 찍힌다고 해요.

가장 좋은 시간은 해 질 무렵이에요. 하늘이 분홍색, 보라색, 오렌지색으로 물들면서 도시가 황금빛으로 빛납니다. 시애틀 사람들이 이 도시를 'Emerald City(에메랄드 시티)'라고 부르는 이유가 그때 비로소 이해돼요.

혹시 레이니어 산이 보이나요? 구름이 없는 날엔 이 거대한 설산이 도시 뒤에 서 있어요. 높이가 4,392미터 — 후지산(3,776m)보다 훨씬 높은 화산이 저기 저렇게 서 있다는 게 믿어지세요? 오늘 이 도시를 이 공원에서 바라보면서, 각자 마음속에 이 순간을 담아두세요.
📍 Pike Place Market
Open since 1907, the oldest continuously operating farmers market in the US. Born as a citizens' revolt against middlemen. The fish-throwing performance started in 1988.
Pike Place Market opened August 17, 1907 — the oldest continuously operating farmers market in the United States. It began as an act of rebellion. Seattle shoppers had to pass through two or three middlemen to buy vegetables, tripling the price by the time food reached the table. Citizens petitioned the city council. The answer: let farmers sell directly. On opening day, ten farmers showed up. By midmorning everything was sold. The crowd was so large police had to manage it.

The bronze pig at the entrance is Rachel — installed in 1986. Drop a coin in and she oinks. All donations fund the market's social services: food bank, medical clinic, senior center, and childcare for market workers. Rachel has raised over $200,000.

The fish throwers at Pike Place Fish started in 1988 — for purely practical reasons. Hauling 30-pound salmon all day was exhausting. Throwing was faster. Tourists started cheering. A performance was born. Now it's a Harvard Business School case study in workplace culture. The principle they teach: when you choose to enjoy your work, everything changes — for you and everyone watching.
📍 Gum Wall (Post Alley)
Started in 1993 when theater-goers began sticking gum while waiting in line. The city cleaned it twice — people kept coming back. Seattle gave up and made it a landmark.
This wall started in 1993 with bored theater-goers waiting in line for the Market Theater next door. Someone stuck their gum. Others followed. The city power-washed it twice. People came back the same evening and started again. So the city surrendered and called it a landmark.

A full cleanup was done in 2015 — they removed approximately one ton of gum. By the following morning, people had already begun restoring it. Some people write wishes on their gum before sticking it. Couples make heart shapes. Kids try to create art. It became a place of unexpected expression — chaotic, colorful, slightly gross, and somehow touching.

There's a philosophy here worth noticing: sometimes the most human things happen when people are just waiting, bored, and decide to leave a small mark on the world. This wall is what happens when millions of people do exactly that.
📍 Space Needle
Built in 400 days for the 1962 World's Fair. One goal: look like the 21st century. Sixty years later, it still does. At 605 feet, it withstands winds up to 200 mph.
The Space Needle was built in just 400 days for the 1962 World's Fair — and the designers had exactly one brief: make it look like the 21st century. It's remarkable that it still does, more than six decades later.

At 605 feet (184 meters), it weighs 9,550 tons — half of that is the foundation. This is earthquake country, and the structure is engineered to survive winds up to 200 mph and a 9.1-magnitude earthquake. In 2018, the observation deck floor was replaced with glass — so you can look straight down at the city below your feet.

At the top: a rotating restaurant called SkyCity that makes one full revolution every 47 minutes. When it opened in 1962, it was the first revolving restaurant in America. On a clear day from the top, you can see Mount Rainier, the Olympic Peninsula, and Vancouver Island in Canada — three different landscapes in one panorama.
📍 Amazon Spheres
Three glass domes housing 40,000 tropical plant species — a working office for Amazon employees. Built on Jeff Bezos's belief that nature boosts creativity.
The Amazon Spheres opened in 2018. Inside three glass domes live over 40,000 plant species from tropical and subtropical regions around the world — Ecuador, Madagascar, the Amazon River basin. Right now, Amazon employees are inside having meetings, writing code, giving presentations — surrounded by living rainforest.

Why build this? Jeff Bezos was convinced that immersion in nature unlocks human creativity in ways an ordinary office cannot. So instead of another glass tower, Amazon built a forest. The plants are maintained by a full-time horticultural team monitoring humidity, temperature, and irrigation 24 hours a day. There's an IT system built specifically for plant care — which feels very Amazon.

The company that built the world's most precise logistics network turned its attention to indoor tropical ecosystem logistics. The result is something that has no parallel anywhere in American corporate architecture.
📍 Kerry Park — Golden Hour
Ninety-five percent of Seattle postcards are shot from here. Space Needle framed by Mount Rainier — at dusk, you finally understand why they call this the Emerald City.
Kerry Park sits on Queen Anne Hill — technically just a strip of lawn with a railing. But it may be the single most photographed spot in the Pacific Northwest. The reason is one specific composition: the Space Needle centered in the frame, downtown towers to the left, Puget Sound to the right, and on clear days, Mount Rainier rising behind everything. Ninety-five percent of Seattle postcards come from exactly here.

The best time to be here is golden hour — the 30 minutes before sunset. The sky turns pink, orange, and violet. The city catches the light. The Sound glitters. Seattle becomes what its nickname promises: the Emerald City.

Is Rainier visible today? On clear days, this 14,411-foot active volcano stands behind the skyline as if placed there deliberately. That a city of skyscrapers and tech campuses exists in the shadow of a volcano — and that the combination is somehow beautiful rather than threatening — tells you something about Seattle's relationship with the natural world it's built inside.

📍 Pioneer Square — 지하 도시
1851년 시작된 시애틀은 1889년 대화재로 전부 불탔습니다. 1년 만에 다시 지으면서 도로 자체를 통째로 들어올렸어요. 그래서 지금 발아래 '지하 도시'가 그대로 남아있습니다.
1851년 11월, 첫 정착민들이 알키 비치에 발을 디뎠어요. 그로부터 37년 뒤인 1889년 6월 6일, 한 가구공장에서 불이 났습니다. 당시 시애틀 소방 호스는 물 압력이 너무 약해서 불을 못 껐어요. 결국 도시 전체 25블록이 전소됐습니다.

놀라운 건 그 다음이에요. 시애틀 사람들은 울지 않았어요. 다음 날부터 재건을 시작했습니다. 그런데 이번엔 똑같이 짓지 않았어요. 저지대였던 Pioneer Square는 조수가 차면 하수가 역류하는 문제가 있었거든요. 그래서 결정했습니다 — 도로 전체를 2층 높이로 올려서 새로 짓자고요.

그 결과, 옛 1층이 그대로 땅 아래 묻혔습니다. 지금도 '시애틀 지하 투어'를 하면 1889년 원래 시애틀 거리를 걸어볼 수 있어요. 120년 된 벽돌, 상점 터, 도로가 그대로 보존되어 있습니다. 불에 탄 도시를 포기하지 않고, 문자 그대로 새 도시를 그 위에 올린 겁니다.
📍 원조 스타벅스
1971년, 세 명의 교사가 커피 원두를 파는 가게로 시작했습니다. 지금의 글로벌 스타벅스를 만든 하워드 슐츠는 창업자가 아니라 나중에 합류한 사람이에요.
1971년, 영문학 교사 제리 볼드윈, 역사 교사 제브 시글, 작가 고든 보커 — 세 친구가 이 가게를 열었습니다. 이름은 소설 『모비딕』에 나오는 커피 좋아하는 항해사 '스타벅'에서 따왔어요. 처음엔 커피를 마시는 곳이 아니었습니다. 고급 커피 원두를 팔고, 고객에게 집에서 직접 내려 마시는 법을 알려주는 가게였어요.

지금의 스타벅스를 만든 하워드 슐츠는 1982년에야 합류했어요. 이탈리아 밀라노를 여행하다가 에스프레소 바 문화에 충격을 받았습니다. '이걸 미국에 가져오면 어떨까?' 창업자들에게 제안했지만 처음엔 거절당했어요. 결국 슐츠는 독자적으로 카페를 열고, 1987년에 스타벅스를 통째로 사들였습니다. 나머지는 역사가 됐죠.

이 가게에서는 다른 스타벅스에서 볼 수 없는 옛날 갈색 로고를 쓰고, 여기서만 파는 한정판 굿즈가 있어요. 긴 줄은 명물입니다.
📍 아마존 창업지 (벨뷰 차고)
1994년 베이조스는 뉴욕 금융사를 그만두고 부인이 운전하는 차 안에서 사업계획서를 썼습니다. 처음 이름 후보는 '리라이언트', '매직', 그리고 최종적으로 세상에서 가장 큰 강 이름 — 아마존.

📍 Pioneer Square — Underground City
Seattle was born in 1851, burned completely in 1889, and was rebuilt in a year — with the streets raised two stories higher. The original city is still down there, underground.
November 1851: the first settlers arrived at Alki Beach. June 6, 1889: a fire started in a cabinet shop. Seattle's water pressure was too weak to fight it. Twenty-five city blocks burned to the ground.

What happened next is the Seattle story. No grieving. The next day, rebuilding began — but not the same as before. Pioneer Square sat in a low-lying tidal zone where sewage backed up into the streets at high tide. Seattle's decision: raise the entire street level two full stories. Build new foundations. Build new ground floors. The old first floors became basements. The old basements became underground.

Today, Seattle Underground Tours walk you through the original 1889 streets — 120-year-old brick walls, shop footprints, original sidewalks preserved below the modern city. Seattle didn't just rebuild. It built a new city on top of the old one, literally. That stubbornness — refusing to accept what was lost — is foundational to how this city thinks.
📍 Original Starbucks
Three teachers opened this store in 1971 — selling coffee beans, not drinks. Howard Schultz, who built the global empire, joined later and bought the company in 1987.
In 1971, English teacher Jerry Baldwin, history teacher Zev Siegl, and writer Gordon Bowker opened a store to sell high-quality coffee beans — not beverages. They named it after Starbuck, the coffee-loving first mate in Moby-Dick. The idea was to teach people to brew great coffee at home.

Howard Schultz joined as marketing director in 1982. He traveled to Milan and was transformed by Italian espresso bar culture — the theater, the ritual, the community of it. He came back and proposed that Starbucks become a café. The founders refused. Schultz opened his own coffee bars instead. In 1987, he bought Starbucks outright from the founders for $3.8 million. The rest is coffee history.

This location carries the original brown logo — the one before the mermaid was cropped and cleaned up. The merchandise here is exclusive: mugs, tumblers, and coffee bags you can only get at this store. The line wraps around the corner every morning. It's worth the wait, if only to say you've been.
📍 Amazon's Origin (Bellevue Garage)
In 1994, Bezos quit his Wall Street job. His wife drove while he wrote the business plan. Name candidates: Relentless, Cadabra, and finally — the world's largest river.
In 1994, Jeff Bezos was a vice president at D.E. Shaw, one of Wall Street's most successful hedge funds. Then he read a statistic: the internet was growing at 2,300% per year. He told his boss he wanted to start an internet company. His boss replied: "That sounds like a great idea for someone who doesn't already have a good job."

Bezos made the decision anyway. His wife MacKenzie drove from New York to Seattle while Jeff wrote the business plan in the passenger seat. First name candidate: Relentless.com. Second: Cadabra — until a lawyer misheard it as "cadaver." Final choice: Amazon. The world's largest river, for a store with the world's largest selection.

July 1994: one garage in Bellevue, three employees, desks made from doors. When the first customer order came in, everyone piled into a car and drove to UPS themselves. The company that garage produced now employs over 1.5 million people worldwide. The door-desks became a company legend — a reminder that frugality and resourcefulness built this, not luxury.
📍 MoPOP (Museum of Pop Culture)
Jimi Hendrix was born in Seattle. Kurt Cobain brought grunge from here to the world. Rain, isolation, cheap warehouses — that combination changed music in the 1990s.
The building itself is a statement. Architect Frank Gehry designed it by digitally fragmenting and reassembling guitar pieces — a visual tribute to Jimi Hendrix, Seattle's most famous musician. Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen, also from Seattle, built this museum to honor Hendrix.

Jimi Hendrix was born in 1942 in Seattle's Central District. His family was poor — he learned to play by practicing on a broom before he could afford a guitar. He died at 27, considered by many the greatest electric guitarist in history. Seattle didn't fully honor him while he was alive. The city is still making up for that.

The 1990s grunge revolution: Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains — all from Seattle. Why here? The rain kept people indoors and introspective. The city was geographically distant from music industry centers, so commercial pressure was low. The economic recession of the 1980s left cheap warehouses where young bands could rehearse for almost nothing. The result: music that sounded like it had nothing to prove and nothing to lose. Every outsider in the world heard it and felt understood.

📍 Indigenous History — Daybreak Star
The Duwamish people lived here for thousands of years before Europeans arrived. Chief Sealth's 1854 words — "How can you buy or sell the sky?" — became a cornerstone of environmentalism.
When I first started leading tours, a question kept nagging at me: whose land was this, before all of this? That question led me here, to the Duwamish story.

For thousands of years before European contact, the Duwamish people fished these waters, built cedar homes on these hills, and considered this land sacred. Then in the 1850s, settlers arrived — and everything changed within a single generation.

In 1854, Chief Sealth spoke to the territorial governor. His words: "How can you buy or sell the sky? The freshness of the air and the sparkle of the water — how do you own them? We do not own the land. We belong to it."

I can't read those words without feeling something. The entire intellectual foundation of modern environmentalism — articulated 150 years ago, right here, by a man whose people had lived it for millennia.

Here's the bittersweet part: Chief Sealth initially refused to let the city use his name. In Duwamish culture, speaking the name of the dead disturbs their spirit. He eventually agreed — reportedly on the condition of an annual payment from the settlers. That payment was never made. His name now appears in every Seattle address, postcard, and souvenir. The city carries the name of a man whose final request was never honored. Lesson: keep your promises.
📍 Chinatown-International District (Our Story Too)
Chinese forced expulsion in the 1880s. Japanese-American internment in 1942. They came back and rebuilt. Korean immigrants came too — and planted roots through tears.
I feel something complicated every time I walk through this neighborhood. Because this is their story — and in many ways, it's our story too.

In the 1880s, Seattle's white residents forcibly loaded Chinese immigrants onto ships and expelled them. These were the men who had built the railroads — the hardest labor, in the worst conditions. When the work was done, they were driven out. In 1942, after Pearl Harbor, Japanese-American families — including people born in America — were sent to internment camps. They lost their homes, businesses, savings, and years they never got back.

They came back. They rebuilt. They are still here. The restaurants and shops in this neighborhood carry the weight of that history in ways you can feel if you know what to look for.

A word especially for our Korean guests: Koreans began arriving in Seattle as early as the 1900s. They worked farms, faced discrimination, built churches, and raised children. Their descendants are now doctors, professors, and business owners in this city. Seeds planted in tears became fruit. Some of you in this group may carry that history in your own families.
📍 Tech Revolution — Gates, Amazon, AI
Microsoft 1979, Amazon 1994, now AI. Three technological revolutions, all from this city. The rain that drove people indoors may have changed the world.
Bill Gates story — it's a good one. In 1968, Gates was a student at Lakeside School in Seattle. The school's mothers' club held a rummage sale and used the money to buy computer time for students. That made Lakeside one of the first high schools in the world with real computer access. Out of all the teenagers on earth at that moment, Gates was one of the almost none who had it.

He became so obsessed that he snuck out of the dorm at night to use the school's computer lab — and got caught. Suspended. (Laughter.) And yet that computer lab, that obsession, that suspension — that's where the world changed. If your child is deeply passionate about something, even something you don't understand yet: pay attention. It might be the seed of the next revolution.

Jeff Bezos. I drive past the Bellevue neighborhood sometimes and think: a single garage, three employees, desks made from doors — and from that, the way the entire world shops changed. Today, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, and Apple all have major operations in Seattle. Engineers from every country on earth are building the next world in this rain.

One lesson I always share here: on rainy days, don't just stay home. Build something. That's what Seattle did.

📍 Alki Beach — Where Seattle Began
In November 1851, 25 settlers landed here in a storm. One woman cried: "This is New York?" They named it Alki — "by and by" in Chinook. That skyline across the water is "by and by" fulfilled.
Every time I walk this beach, I try to imagine November 1851.

Twenty-five settlers arrived by boat in a storm — rain, wind, waves. They stepped onto this sand, looked around at dense forest and grey water, and someone reportedly started to cry. A woman said, in frustration: "They told us this would be like New York." (Laughter.)

The group decided to call their new home Alki — a word from Chinook jargon meaning "by and by." Not New York yet. Someday.

Now look across the water. That skyline — the skyscrapers, the Amazon Spheres, the Space Needle — that's "by and by." One hundred and seventy years later. Built from a beach landing in a storm by 25 people who had nothing but the audacity to try.

Seattle did eventually become the city they dreamed of — just not the one they imagined. It became something better and stranger and more specific.

The lesson I always take from this beach: the "by and by" you're working toward may not look like what you pictured. But it may be more interesting. Don't let the storm convince you to go home.
📍 Green Lake — Where Seattle Breathes

Green Lake is the neighborhood where Seattle exhales. The 2.8-mile path around this lake is one of the most consistently used pieces of public space in the Pacific Northwest. Every morning, every evening — runners, cyclists, dog-walkers, elderly couples, young parents with strollers.

What I love about Green Lake is that it refuses to sort people by speed or status. An Amazon engineer in technical gear passes a grandmother feeding ducks. A meditation practitioner does slow tai chi while teenagers skateboard nearby. The lake accepts all of them at their own pace.

The whole city seems to be optimizing for something. But here, no one is optimizing. They're just walking around a lake. And somehow, that feels radical.

I come here alone sometimes after a long tour day — just one slow loop, watching the light change on the water. It's where I remember that the city isn't just the story I tell about it. It's also this: ordinary people, moving slowly, taking up space, being alive without any particular agenda.

그때 어떤 사람이 시내 광고판에 이런 글을 붙였어요: "시애틀을 떠나는 마지막 사람 — 불 꺼줘 주세요."

웃기죠? 근데 그게 얼마나 절망적인 상황인지도 느껴지죠? 이 광고판이 시애틀 역사에서 가장 유명한 문장 중 하나가 됐어요. 시애틀의 어두운 유머, 자조적인 위트가 담겨 있어요.

그런데 불은 꺼지지 않았어요. 1975년 마이크로소프트가 왔고, 1994년 아마존이 창고에서 시작했고, 스타벅스가 전 세계로 뻗어나갔어요. 교훈: 가장 어두운 밤이 지나야 새벽이 옵니다. 시애틀이 그 살아있는 증거예요.
William Boeing built planes in a lakeside boathouse in 1916. In 1971, 60,000 workers were laid off. A billboard appeared: "Will the last person leaving Seattle please turn out the lights." The lights stayed on.
I love this story. William Boeing — a timber man by trade — started building airplanes in 1916 in a boathouse on Lake Union. Wood frame, fabric skin. He and his friend George Conrad Westervelt built the first plane by hand in that building. You can visit the site today — it's a maritime heritage museum now.

Boeing grew through World War II, manufacturing the B-17 and B-29 bombers. By the 1960s, Boeing was Seattle. Half the city's economy ran through that company. If you worked in Seattle, chances were you knew someone at Boeing.

Then 1971 arrived. Aerospace industry collapse, recession, government contract cancellations. Boeing laid off 60,000 workers. Sixty thousand people. In a city that size, that's not just an economic statistic — that's neighbors, church members, your kids' friends' parents, all losing their jobs at once.

Someone put up a billboard on the highway out of town: "Will the last person leaving Seattle please turn out the lights."

It's funny. And devastating. That combination — humor in the face of real pain — is very Seattle. The billboard became one of the most famous images in the city's history.

But the lights didn't go out. Microsoft came. Amazon came. Starbucks went global. The people who stayed rebuilt something different — and better. Sometimes a city, like a person, has to lose what it thought defined it before it discovers what it actually is.

파이크 플레이스 마켓에서 연어 보셨죠? 시애틀을 진짜 이해하려면 저 연어를 이해해야 해요.

연어의 일생이 놀라워요. 민물 강에서 태어나서, 바다로 내려가요. 태평양에서 2~5년을 삽니다. 그러다 어느 날 — 자기가 태어난 바로 그 강으로 돌아와요. 수백, 때로는 수천 킬로미터를 거슬러서요. 도중에 폭포도 뛰어 넘어요. 그리고 알을 낳고, 죽습니다.

어떻게 찾아올까요? 과학자들은 후각이라고 해요. 태어난 강의 물 냄새 — 정확히는 물 속 미네랄과 미생물의 화학적 구성을 기억해요. 같은 강의 지류도 구별할 정도로 정확해요. 수년간 태평양을 떠돌다가, 고향 냄새를 맡고 돌아오는 거예요.

드와미시 원주민들에게 연어는 단순한 음식이 아니었어요. 신성한 존재였습니다. '돌아오는 것' — 아무리 멀리 가도, 처음 시작한 곳으로 반드시 돌아온다는 것. 이게 이 땅의 철학이에요.

저는 이 이야기를 할 때마다 이런 질문이 생겨요. 나는 어디서 왔고, 어디로 돌아가야 할까? 여러분은 어떠세요? 아무리 멀리 가도 결국 내가 돌아가야 할 곳이 있다면 — 그곳이 어디인가요?
Salmon are born in a river, migrate to the ocean for years, then return to the exact stream where they were born — guided by smell memory. To Indigenous peoples, the salmon embodied the philosophy of return.
You saw the salmon at Pike Place Market. To really understand Seattle, you need to understand that fish.

A salmon's life is extraordinary. Born in a freshwater river. Migrates downstream to the ocean. Spends two to five years in the open Pacific — thousands of miles from home. Then one day, something pulls it back. It returns to the exact river, the exact tributary, the exact stretch of water where it was born. Hundreds, sometimes thousands of miles upstream. It leaps waterfalls. It fights the current. It arrives, spawns, and dies.

How does it find its way? Scientists say smell memory. The salmon memorizes the precise chemical signature of its birth water — the specific minerals, the specific microorganisms — and navigates back across an ocean using that memory alone. Accurate enough to distinguish between two branches of the same river.

To the Duwamish and other Indigenous peoples of this region, salmon were sacred. Not just food — sacred. The return of the salmon every year was a ceremony, a reminder that what goes out comes back. That no matter how far you travel, the beginning calls you home.

I think about this every time I tell it: where did I come from, and where am I supposed to return to? After all your travels, your career, your years of building — is there something you're being called back toward? The salmon doesn't ask whether it's worth it. It just goes home.

제가 시애틀 사람들한테 물어봤어요. "무섭지 않아요?" 대부분 이렇게 답해요. "마지막 대규모 폭발이 1,000년 전이에요. 경보 시스템 있어요. 그리고..." 그리고 어깨 한번 으쓱. (웃음)

그게 시애틀 사람들이에요. 화산 옆에 살면서 아름답다고 감탄하는 사람들. 위험을 알면서도 여기서 사는 이유가 그 위험보다 이 아름다움이 더 크기 때문이에요. 우리 인생도 그렇지 않나요? 위험 없는 삶은 아름답지 않아요.
That beautiful snow-capped mountain is an active volcano. The USGS calls it "America's most dangerous volcano." An eruption would send lahars — volcanic mudflows — at 40mph through valleys where 150,000+ people live. Seattleites respond with a shrug.
See that gorgeous snow-capped mountain? Mount Rainier. One of my favorite moments on this tour is what happens next — because I haven't told you yet. That mountain is an active volcano. (Pause for effect.)

The United States Geological Survey officially calls Mount Rainier "one of the most dangerous volcanoes in the world." Here's why: it stands 14,411 feet high, and from summit to mid-slope, it's covered in glaciers — more glacier ice than any other mountain in the lower 48 states. If it erupts, the heat instantly melts those glaciers. That water mixes with volcanic rock and ash into a lahar — a volcanic mudflow — moving at 40 miles per hour down every valley leading away from the mountain.

The population in those valleys: over 150,000 people. Towns like Tacoma, Auburn, and Puyallup sit in the direct path. Scientists estimate that residents would have approximately 30 minutes to evacuate after an alarm is triggered.

I've asked Seattle locals: "Doesn't that scare you?" Most of them say: "Last major eruption was about 1,000 years ago. We have monitoring systems. And—" and then a shrug. (Laughter.)

That's the Seattle personality. Living next to a volcano and still calling it beautiful. Knowing the risk and choosing to stay because the beauty is worth it. There's something honest about that — a city that decided the reward of this view is worth the risk of what it's attached to. Isn't that true of most things worth having?

1968. Bill Gates is 13, a student at Lakeside School in Seattle. The school mothers' club runs a rummage sale and uses the proceeds to buy time on a time-sharing computer. Gates was one of a tiny handful of adolescents who could actually use one.

Think about that. Two boys in one computer room in Seattle. Without that room, no Microsoft. Without Microsoft, no ecosystem that attracted Amazon. Without Amazon and Microsoft together, Seattle doesn't become the tech capital it is today.

Microsoft's success reshaped Seattle's economy. That drew in Amazon. Amazon's success drew Google, Meta, Apple, and Nvidia. Seattle is now one of the world's centers for AI research and development. The lesson I always take from this: you cannot know which room your child walks into will change everything. Pay attention to what ignites them — and give them access to it.

Sleepless in Seattle (1993) — Tom Hanks, Meg Ryan. This single film reshaped Seattle's tourist identity. Space Needle visitor numbers jumped after the release. The houseboat on Lake Union where Hanks's character lived became one of the most photographed private residences in the city. It's an actual home with real residents — who've long since given up trying to keep fans off the dock. (Laughter.)

Grey's Anatomy — one of the longest-running medical dramas in TV history, set at the fictional "Seattle Grace Hospital," modeled on UW Medical Center. Filmed entirely in LA — but the show gave Seattle a global reputation as a city of brilliant, emotionally complicated surgeons. Enrollment inquiries at UW Medical reportedly spiked after the show's peak years.

Twin Peaks — David Lynch's landmark series opens with Snoqualmie Falls. If you're visiting the falls tomorrow, you'll recognize it immediately. That waterfall image became one of the most iconic opening sequences in television history.

10 Things I Hate About You — filmed at Gas Works Park and Stadium High School in Tacoma. A modern Shakespeare adaptation, set against Seattle's industrial waterfront backdrop.

Why does Hollywood keep choosing Seattle? The shorthand works: rain, introspection, beauty with melancholy underneath. Characters in Seattle are allowed to feel things — complicated things. The city gives emotional permission that sunnier settings don't. And honestly? Having lived here, I can confirm: the shorthand is accurate.

Why does Seattle get so much rain?
Seattle actually gets less annual rainfall than New York — 37 inches versus 45. But Seattle is overcast or drizzling more than 150 days a year. It rains gently, constantly, persistently.

The Olympic Mountains trap Pacific moisture — Seattle sits right on the edge of that rain shadow. That's why locals don't carry umbrellas. If you see someone with one in Seattle, they're probably visiting from out of town.

Extremely expensive. A one-bedroom apartment averages $2,500 to $3,000 per month. The median home price is around $800,000 to $900,000.

Why do Seattleites seem friendly but hard to get close to?
'The Seattle Freeze' — it has an actual name, and Seattleites acknowledge it themselves.

On the surface: extremely polite. Great small talk. But that warmth rarely deepens into real friendship. Seattleites value quality over quantity in relationships. They won't fake closeness. But once you earn a Seattleite's real friendship, it tends to be honest and lasting. The freeze is a long front door, not an empty house.

5Walt Crowley — National Trust Guide: Seattle1998건축, 파이크 플레이스파이크 플레이스 마켓 역사 상세 기술.
6Mark Yarm — Everybody Loves Our Town: An Oral History of Grunge2011록 음악, MoPOP너바나·펄 잼·사운드가든 당사자 인터뷰 모음. 그런지 시대 생생한 기록.
7Charles R. Cross — Heavier Than Heaven: A Biography of Kurt Cobain2001커트 코베인 스크립트코베인 생애 가장 권위 있는 전기. 시애틀·애버딘 배경 상세.

2Hyung-chan Kim — The Korean Diaspora1977하와이·미 본토 한인 이민 1세대 연구.
3Wing Luke Museum of the Asian Pacific American Experience (시애틀 소재)상설시애틀 아시아계 이민사 상설 전시. 한인 이민 섹션 포함. 실제 방문 추천.
4SCIDpda (Chinatown-International District Preservation and Development Authority)온라인차이나타운-인터내셔널 디스트릭트 공식 역사 자료. scidpda.org
5First Korean United Methodist Church of Seattle — 교회 기록1904~미 서부 최초 한인 교회 중 하나. 시애틀 한인 이민 신앙 공동체의 시작.
6Ronald Takaki — Strangers from a Different Shore: A History of Asian Americans1989중국·일본·한국·필리핀계 이민사 통합 서술. 차별과 저항의 역사.
7Pyong Gap Min — Caught in the Middle: Korean Communities in New York and Los Angeles19961992 LA 폭동과 한인 커뮤니티. 미국 내 한인 정체성 연구.
📌 시애틀 한인 이민사 핵심 사실 (각주용):
• 한인의 미국 이민은 1903년 하와이 사탕수수 농장 노동자로 시작. 이후 캘리포니아·워싱턴주로 이동.
• 시애틀 지역 한인 이민자들은 농장 노동, 어업, 캐너리(통조림 공장) 등에 종사. 혹독한 차별 속에서도 교회 중심으로 공동체 형성.
• 1992년 LA 폭동 이후 한인 커뮤니티 정체성 재정립. 시애틀은 상대적으로 안정된 한인 커뮤니티 성장.
• 현재 시애틀 광역권 한인 인구 약 5만~7만 명 추산. 벨뷰·페더럴웨이·렌턴 등에 집중.

• 골드러시·아마존 → 탐심과 청지기직. 눅 12:15. "삶이 소유의 풍족함에 있지 않음."
• 음악·창조문화 → 일반 은혜(Common Grace). 하나님이 모든 인류에게 창조 능력 부여.
• 연어의 귀환 → 탕자의 귀환(눅 15). 본향으로 돌아오는 인간의 본능.
• 화산 위의 도시 → 불확실성 속 신뢰. 시 46:2-3. "땅이 변하고 산이 흔들려도."
